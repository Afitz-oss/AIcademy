# What Is a Large Language Model?

A large language model (LLM) is a neural network trained on enormous amounts of text to predict the next token in a sequence. By learning to predict what comes next, it absorbs grammar, facts, reasoning patterns, and writing styles.

An LLM does not "look things up." It generates text one token at a time, each token chosen based on the probability distribution the model computes over its vocabulary. This is why LLMs can be fluent yet occasionally wrong — they are predicting plausible continuations, not retrieving verified facts.

Modern LLMs like GPT-4 and Claude are based on the transformer architecture. They are trained in two main phases: pretraining on broad text, then fine-tuning (often with human feedback) to make them helpful and safe to interact with.
